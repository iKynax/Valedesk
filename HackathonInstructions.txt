What we’re looking for
We care about how you think, not just what you build.
Functionality: Does it work? Does it solve the problem it set out to solve?
Code quality: Is your code clean, readable, and reasonably structured? We're not expecting
perfection, we're looking for good habits.
Problem-solving: How did you handle edge cases, tricky decisions, or things that didn't go as
planned?
Communication: Can you explain what you built, why you made the choices you made, and
what you'd improve with more time? This matters as much as the code itself.
Tech stack is your call. Web, mobile, desktop—use whatever you're comfortable with.

FAQ
1. Submission Requirements
Q: Do we need to build a new project, or can we submit a previous one?
You must build a new project from scratch.
Q: Who will own the work?
You will have full ownership of everything you build.
This is your project. You’re free to use it as a portfolio piece for future applications,
continue improving it after the challenge, or even turn it into something bigger.
Q: Do we need to submit both documentation and a working prototype? What
exactly should be included?
Yes. You are required to submit both a functional prototype and supporting documentation.
By the end of the two-week build period, you should have a working product that you can
showcase during your demo. Your submission should demonstrate not only what you built, but
how you approached the problem.
Your final submission must include:
1. Functional Prototype
● A working version of your app (PLC/prototype)
● A deployed build if possible (e.g., Vercel, Netlify)
2. Codebase
● A link to your GitHub/GitLab/Bitbucket repo
● Complex logic clearly documented in the repository
3. Demo Video
● A short walkthrough of your app
● 3–5 minutes is recommended, but not a strict limit
● Focus on features, functionality, and your process
4. Documentation (How in-depth should it be?)
Documentation should be 1–2 pages and focused on your thought process
Suggested elements to include:
● How you planned and approached the app
● Why you chose certain tools, frameworks, or libraries
● Your main technical decisions and reasoning
● 1–2 flowcharts for key feature flows
● A simple technical architecture overview
● The tech stack used
Q: Do we have to follow the suggested topics?
No, the provided topics are just examples. You’re free to use them or propose your own
idea, as long as it fits the overall theme of the program and can be built within the given
timeframe.
Q: Is there a preferred tech stack or framework?/ Is there a required programming
language (e.g., Java, Python)?
No strict requirement.
● TypeScript is recommended
● Use whatever framework or stack you’re comfortable with
● Ensure you can deliver within the timeline
Q: Are two key features enough?
Yes. Two main key features are sufficient, but you may add more if it strengthens your
app and is feasible within the two-week timeframe. Focus on impactful, realistic features
rather than overly complex ones.
You are free to enhance your app with:
● Additional key features
● Better UI/UX
● Unique selling points
● Creative ideas
Q: Can the app be a CLI instead of a GUI?
Yes. Mobile, web, desktop, or CLI are all acceptable.
Justify your choice by explaining:
● The use case
● Who the target user is
● Why the chosen interface makes sense
Q: Should we go deeper or broader in functionality?
Go deeper, not broader. Because the timeline is short, it’s better to build 2 solid,
well-developed features rather than 10 half-baked features
Q: Can we build both a web app and a complementary mobile app?
Yes, as long as you can complete it within the deadline, multiple platforms are allowed.
2. Presentation and Demo
Q: How long should the video demo be?
3–5 minutes would be ideal, but there’s no strict limit. Focus on clearly presenting:
● What you built
● How it works
● Why you made certain decisions
Q: Do we need to show our face in the recorded demo?
No, face is optional. A voice-over is recommended for clarity.
Q: How do I submit the video?
You may upload your video to:
● YouTube (unlisted), or
● Google Drive
Then share the link in your submission.
3. Evaluation Criteria
Q: What carries the most weight in scoring?
Projects will be judged on three main levels:
1. Code level
We’ll review your source code for structure, clarity, problem-solving logic, and
implementation quality. Submit a GitHub repo and make sure complex logic is
documented.
2. Product level
We’ll assess how well your app functions and whether it solves the chosen
problem effectively. Creative and user-centered solutions are encouraged.
3. Presentation and documentation
We’ll look at how effectively you communicate your ideas. This includes your
ability to present the project clearly and your supporting documentation that helps
us understand your thinking and execution.
Q: Is the evaluation focused purely on technical functionality, or will UX and
design also play a role?
Ultimately, we build products—so functionality is key, and you’ll need to show strong
technical ability. But usability and the overall user experience are just as important. So
yes, we evaluate both the technical side and how well the product works for users.
Q: Will we score higher if we follow the suggested topics?
Not necessarily.
The five topics are examples. You may:
● Build your own topic
● Propose something entirely different
● Choose any idea that you can complete in time
All submissions, whether based on suggested topics or self-proposed ones, are
evaluated the same way.
Q: Does picking your own topic carry extra weight?
No extra weight. However, creativity is one of the evaluation criteria, so unique
problem-solving can help you stand out.
